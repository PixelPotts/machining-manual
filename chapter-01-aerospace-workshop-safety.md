## 1.1 Introduction to Aerospace Safety Standards

### Why Aerospace Safety is Different
Aerospace manufacturing operates under the most stringent safety and quality standards in industry. A single failure can result in catastrophic loss of life and property. As a senior machinist and programmer, you are responsible for:

- **Product Safety**: Your work directly affects aircraft safety
- **Personnel Safety**: Protecting yourself and coworkers from harm
- **Environmental Compliance**: Proper handling of hazardous materials
- **Regulatory Compliance**: Meeting FAA, EASA, and company standards

### Key Safety Mindset Principles
1. **Zero Tolerance for Shortcuts**: Never compromise safety for speed or convenience
2. **Question Everything**: If something doesn't feel right, stop and investigate
3. **Continuous Vigilance**: Maintain awareness of changing conditions
4. **Personal Responsibility**: You are accountable for your actions and their consequences

## 1.3 Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

### Mandatory PPE for All Shop Operations

#### Safety Glasses
**Requirement**: ANSI Z87.1+ rated safety glasses at all times in shop
**Types:**
- Clear polycarbonate for general use
- Tinted for bright welding/grinding operations
- Side shields required for all operations
- Prescription safety glasses available for vision correction

**Inspection Procedure:**
1. Check for cracks, scratches, or damage before each shift
2. Verify ANSI marking is present and legible
3. Ensure proper fit - no gaps at temples or nose
4. Replace immediately if damaged

#### Hearing Protection
**Requirement**: Required in all areas exceeding 85 dB
**Types:**
- Foam earplugs (NRR 29-33 dB): Single use, proper insertion critical
- Silicone earplugs (NRR 25-27 dB): Reusable, washable
- Over-ear muffs (NRR 25-30 dB): Best for intermittent exposure
- Electronic muffs: Allow communication while protecting

**Proper Insertion for Foam Plugs:**
1. Roll plug between clean fingers to compress
2. Pull ear up and back with opposite hand
3. Insert compressed plug with slight twisting motion
4. Hold for 30 seconds while plug expands
5. Plug should not be visible when properly inserted

#### Steel-Toed Safety Shoes
**Requirement**: ANSI Z41 or ASTM F2413 rated footwear
**Features Required:**
- Steel or composite toe protection (75 ft-lbs impact, 2500 lbs compression)
- Slip-resistant soles
- Electrical hazard protection (EH rated)
- Puncture-resistant soles for metal chip areas

**Daily Inspection:**
- Check sole integrity for embedded metal chips
- Verify laces are in good condition
- Inspect for oil/chemical damage to leather/synthetic materials
- Ensure toe protection has no cracks or deformation

### Operation-Specific PPE

#### CNC Machine Operations
**Required:**
- Safety glasses (always)
- Hearing protection (if noise >85 dB)
- Close-fitting clothing (no loose sleeves, ties, jewelry)
- Steel-toed shoes
- Cut-resistant gloves when handling sharp parts or tools

**Prohibited:**
- Loose clothing that can catch in rotating machinery
- Jewelry (rings, watches, chains)
- Long hair not contained
- Open-toed shoes or sandals

#### Manual Machine Operations
**Additional Requirements:**
- Cut-resistant gloves (ANSI A3 minimum) for part handling
- Face shield for grinding operations
- Apron for coolant splash protection
- Knee pads for floor-level work

#### Material-Specific PPE Requirements

**Titanium Machining:**
- Fire-resistant clothing (Nomex or equivalent)
- Full face shield with respirator capability
- Specialized fire extinguisher within reach
- Emergency shower access verified

**Beryllium Copper:**
- NIOSH-approved respirator (P100 filter minimum)
- Disposable coveralls
- Nitrile gloves (double layer recommended)
- Dedicated work area with negative pressure ventilation

**Carbon Fiber Composites:**
- Respirator with P100 filters
- Cut-resistant gloves (carbon fiber splinters)
- Long sleeves to prevent skin contact
- Eye protection (particles can cause severe irritation)

## 1.4 Machine-Specific Safety Requirements

### CNC Machining Center Safety

#### Pre-Operation Safety Checks
**Every Time Before Starting:**
1. **Emergency Stop Function Test**
   - Press E-stop button
   - Verify all motion stops immediately
   - Verify spindle stops within 3 seconds
   - Test E-stop at both operator panel and remote locations
   - Document test in daily log

2. **Door Interlock Verification**
   - Open each access door
   - Verify machine stops all motion
   - Attempt to start spindle with door open (should be impossible)
   - Check door seals for coolant containment

3. **Chuck/Vise Security Check**
   - Verify all clamps are tight (use torque wrench if specified)
   - Check that workpiece is properly seated
   - Ensure no tools or loose objects in work area
   - Verify chip auger/conveyor operation

#### During Operation Safety
**Continuous Monitoring Requirements:**
- Never leave machine unattended during first part of new program
- Monitor coolant level and pressure
- Listen for abnormal sounds (bearing noise, tool chatter, etc.)
- Watch for excessive heat buildup or smoke
- Verify chip evacuation is functioning

**Prohibited Actions During Operation:**
- Opening doors while spindle is running (even with override)
- Reaching into work area while machine is in cycle
- Using compressed air to clean work area with spindle running
- Adjusting workholding while machine is in motion

#### Tool Change Safety
**Automatic Tool Changer (ATC):**
1. Always use "Single Block" mode for first tool change verification
2. Ensure tool carousel path is clear of obstructions
3. Verify proper tool orientation before loading into magazine
4. Never manually move ATC arm while in automatic mode
5. Always verify tool length and diameter before use

**Manual Tool Changes:**
1. Ensure spindle is stopped and locked
2. Use proper tool holders - never force incompatible tools
3. Clean spindle taper and tool holder taper before insertion
4. Verify tool is properly seated and draw bar is tight
5. Update tool offset table immediately

### Lathe Safety Requirements

#### Chuck Safety
**Chuck Jaw Inspection (Daily):**
1. Remove jaws and inspect for wear, cracks, or damage
2. Check jaw slides for proper fit and smooth operation
3. Verify chuck key fits properly with no excessive play
4. Lubricate jaw slides according to manufacturer schedule
5. Test chuck grip strength with test bar

**Chuck Key Safety:**
- Remove chuck key immediately after use
- Never leave chuck key in chuck
- Use spring-loaded chuck keys when available
- Store chuck key in designated location only

#### Tailstock Safety
1. Ensure tailstock is locked before operation
2. Verify center alignment for between-centers work
3. Check tailstock quill extension limits
4. Lubricate centers and verify smooth rotation

## 1.5 Material-Specific Safety Protocols

### Aluminum Alloys
**Primary Hazards:**
- Aluminum dust (explosion/fire risk)
- Sharp edges and burrs
- Chip entanglement

**Safety Requirements:**
- Flood coolant recommended for high-speed operations
- Chip guards in place and functioning
- Regular cleaning to prevent chip buildup
- Cut-resistant gloves for part handling

**Chip Management:**
- Never allow aluminum chips to accumulate
- Use vacuum system, not compressed air for cleaning
- Store aluminum chips in designated, covered containers
- Keep chips away from heat sources and electrical equipment

### Titanium Alloys
**Primary Hazards:**
- Fire risk (titanium burns in air above 550°F)
- Toxic fumes when burning
- Work hardening under improper cutting conditions

**Mandatory Safety Requirements:**
1. **Fire Prevention:**
   - Flood coolant mandatory (never run dry)
   - Sharp tools only (dull tools create heat)
   - Continuous chip removal
   - Class D fire extinguisher within 25 feet

2. **Emergency Procedures:**
   - If titanium fire occurs: evacuate area, call fire department
   - Never use water or standard extinguishers on titanium fires
   - Use sand or specialized Class D extinguisher only

3. **Ventilation Requirements:**
   - Local exhaust ventilation for all titanium operations
   - Air changes: minimum 6 per hour
   - Filtration: HEPA filters for particulate removal

### Stainless Steel
**Primary Hazards:**
- Hexavalent chromium in dust/fumes
- Work hardening
- Galling and seizing

**Safety Requirements:**
- Respirator required for dry machining operations
- Local exhaust ventilation recommended
- Proper cutting parameters to prevent work hardening
- Sulfur-based cutting oils prohibited (stress corrosion)

### Inconel and Superalloys
**Primary Hazards:**
- High cutting temperatures
- Rapid tool wear
- Toxic metal particulates (cobalt, chromium)

**Safety Requirements:**
- Flood coolant mandatory
- Respirator with P100 filters for dry operations
- Regular tool inspection (broken tools create heat)
- Local exhaust ventilation required

### Beryllium Copper
**PRIMARY SAFETY WARNING: BERYLLIUM IS CARCINOGENIC**

**Mandatory Safety Requirements:**
1. **Engineering Controls:**
   - Dedicated work area with negative pressure
   - HEPA filtration on all exhaust air
   - Wet cutting methods only (never dry machine)
   - Sealed waste containers for all chips and debris

2. **Personal Protection:**
   - NIOSH-approved respirator (P100 minimum)
   - Disposable coveralls
   - Double nitrile gloves
   - Disposable shoe covers

3. **Decontamination:**
   - Wash hands and face before eating, drinking, smoking
   - Change clothes before leaving work area
   - Shower recommended at end of shift
   - Contaminated clothing must be professionally laundered

4. **Medical Surveillance:**
   - Annual chest X-rays
   - Pulmonary function testing
   - Medical removal if beryllium sensitization detected

## 1.6 Chemical and Coolant Safety

### Coolant Safety

#### Types of Coolant and Associated Hazards
**Straight Cutting Oils:**
- Hazards: Skin contact dermatitis, fire risk, vapor inhalation
- PPE: Nitrile gloves, apron, ventilation
- Fire classification: Class B fire (use CO2 or foam extinguisher)

**Water-Soluble Coolants (Synthetic/Semi-Synthetic):**
- Hazards: Bacterial growth, skin sensitization, eye irritation
- PPE: Safety glasses, gloves when handling concentrate
- pH monitoring: Maintain 8.8-9.2 to prevent bacterial growth

**Minimum Quantity Lubrication (MQL):**
- Hazards: Mist inhalation, skin contact
- Ventilation: Local exhaust required
- PPE: Respirator if mist generation is high

#### Daily Coolant Safety Procedures
**Morning Inspection:**
1. Check coolant level and concentration (refractometer)
2. Visual inspection for contamination or bacterial growth
3. pH testing with calibrated meter
4. Temperature check (should be <90°F for extended life)
5. Skimming of tramp oils

**Weekly Coolant Management:**
1. Bacterial testing (dip slide method)
2. Complete system cleaning if bacterial count >10^6 CFU/ml
3. Filter replacement/cleaning
4. Concentration adjustment based on usage
5. System flushing if contaminated

#### Coolant Disposal
**Environmental Compliance:**
- Never discharge coolant to drains or soil
- Used coolant is hazardous waste - follow EPA guidelines
- Use licensed waste disposal contractor
- Maintain disposal records for 5 years

### Cutting Fluid MSDS Requirements

**Information You Must Know:**
1. **Chemical Composition**: Understand what chemicals you're exposed to
2. **Health Hazards**: Acute and chronic health effects
3. **First Aid Measures**: Know what to do for skin/eye contact
4. **Fire Fighting Measures**: Proper extinguisher types
5. **Spill Cleanup**: How to safely clean up spills
6. **Storage Requirements**: Temperature, separation from incompatible materials

**MSDS Location and Access:**
- Physical copies at each workstation using the chemical
- Electronic copies on company intranet
- 24-hour emergency contact numbers posted
- Updated within 30 days of chemical changes

## 1.7 Fire Safety and Prevention

### Fire Classifications and Suppression

#### Class A Fires (Ordinary Combustibles)
- **Materials**: Wood, paper, plastic, fabric
- **Extinguisher**: Water, foam, multipurpose dry chemical
- **Shop Sources**: Packaging materials, shop rags, wooden fixtures

#### Class B Fires (Flammable Liquids)
- **Materials**: Cutting oils, hydraulic fluids, solvents, gasoline
- **Extinguisher**: Foam, CO2, dry chemical (never water)
- **Shop Sources**: Coolant, hydraulic systems, parts cleaners

#### Class C Fires (Electrical)
- **Materials**: Energized electrical equipment
- **Extinguisher**: CO2, dry chemical (never water or foam)
- **Shop Sources**: Machine control panels, motors, wiring

#### Class D Fires (Combustible Metals)
- **Materials**: Magnesium, titanium, aluminum powder, lithium
- **Extinguisher**: Special dry powder (sand as emergency backup)
- **Shop Sources**: Titanium machining, magnesium parts, metal powders

### Fire Prevention Strategies

#### Housekeeping Requirements
**Daily:**
- Remove all combustible waste (chips, rags, paper)
- Clean up spilled oils and coolants immediately
- Ensure electrical panels are free of metal chips
- Verify fire extinguishers are accessible and charged

**Weekly:**
- Inspect electrical connections for heat damage
- Check for oil leaks on machines
- Verify emergency exits are clear and marked
- Test fire suppression system (if automated)

#### Hot Work Procedures
**Definition**: Any work producing sparks, flames, or heat >300°F
**Examples**: Grinding, welding, torch cutting, heat treating

**Hot Work Permit Required For:**
1. All welding and cutting operations
2. Grinding operations producing sparks
3. Heat treating or stress relieving
4. Any work with open flames

**Hot Work Safety Requirements:**
1. **30-Foot Rule**: Remove all combustible materials within 30 feet
2. **Fire Watch**: Trained person with appropriate extinguisher
3. **Pre-Work Inspection**: Document area safety before starting
4. **Post-Work Monitoring**: 30 minutes after work completion

### Emergency Response Procedures

#### Fire Emergency Response
**Upon Discovering Fire:**
1. **RACE Protocol**:
   - **R**escue: Remove people from immediate danger
   - **A**larm: Activate fire alarm system
   - **C**onfine: Close doors to contain fire (if safe)
   - **E**xtinguish: Fight fire only if trained and safe

**Never Fight Fire If:**
- Fire is larger than you can handle with available extinguisher
- Toxic materials involved (beryllium, plastics)
- Electrical equipment energized and cannot be de-energized
- Exit route is threatened

#### Evacuation Procedures
**Primary Route**: Main entrance/exit
**Secondary Route**: Emergency exit (rear of building)
**Assembly Point**: Parking lot northwest corner (150 feet from building)
**Roll Call**: Department supervisors account for all personnel

## 1.8 Electrical Safety

### Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Procedures
**When LOTO Required:**
- Maintenance or repair of machinery
- Clearing jams or blockages
- Any work inside electrical panels
- Tool changes requiring guard removal
- Calibration of safety devices

#### Step-by-Step LOTO Procedure

**Step 1: Preparation**
1. Identify all energy sources (electrical, pneumatic, hydraulic)
2. Locate all isolation devices (breakers, valves, switches)
3. Gather personal locks and tags (one per energy source)
4. Notify affected personnel of shutdown

**Step 2: Machine Shutdown**
1. Complete current machining cycle if safe
2. Turn off machine at normal operating controls
3. Allow machine to come to complete stop
4. Verify all motion has ceased

**Step 3: Isolation**
1. Disconnect main electrical power at breaker/disconnect
2. Close pneumatic isolation valves
3. Close hydraulic isolation valves
4. Block any potential gravitational motion

**Step 4: Lockout Application**
1. Apply personal lock to each isolation device
2. Attach personal tag with:
   - Your name and department
   - Date and time of application
   - Reason for lockout
   - Expected completion time

**Step 5: Energy Verification**
1. Attempt to start machine at normal controls (should not start)
2. Test electrical circuits with meter (should read zero)
3. Verify hydraulic pressure is at zero
4. Verify pneumatic pressure is at zero
5. Dissipate stored energy (capacitors, accumulators)

**Step 6: Work Performance**
- Perform necessary work safely
- Maintain locks throughout work period
- Do not remove locks to test - complete work first

**Step 7: Restoration**
1. Remove all tools and materials from work area
2. Replace all guards and safety devices
3. Clear all personnel from danger areas
4. Remove tags first, then personal locks
5. Restore energy sources in reverse order of isolation
6. Test machine operation for normal function

#### Personal Lock Requirements
- **Unique Key**: Each person has unique lock and key
- **Durable Construction**: Able to withstand shop environment
- **Identification**: Employee name clearly marked
- **Color Coding**: Department-specific colors (optional but recommended)

#### Tag Information Requirements
- **Employee name and signature**
- **Date and time applied**
- **Reason for lockout**
- **Department and shift**
- **Expected duration of work**
- **Emergency contact information**

### Electrical Safety Inspection

#### Daily Inspection Items
1. **Power Cords and Plugs**:
   - No damaged insulation or exposed wires
   - Plugs fit snugly in outlets
   - No extension cords for permanent equipment
   - Ground prongs intact and not removed

2. **Electrical Panels**:
   - Covers in place and secured
   - No metal chips or debris inside
   - Panel labels clear and accurate
   - No moisture or coolant contamination

3. **Machine Electrical Systems**:
   - No burning smell or unusual odors
   - No sparking at connections
   - Emergency stops functioning properly
   - All lights and indicators working

#### Electrical Hazard Recognition
**Immediate Danger Signs:**
- Sparking at electrical connections
- Burning smell from electrical equipment
- Electrical panels hot to touch
- Lights flickering during machine operation
- Circuit breakers tripping repeatedly

**Action Required:**
1. De-energize equipment immediately
2. Tag equipment "OUT OF SERVICE"
3. Report to maintenance immediately
4. Do not attempt repairs unless qualified

### Arc Flash Safety
**Arc Flash Definition**: Explosive release of electrical energy through air

**Protection Requirements:**
- Arc flash analysis required for electrical work
- Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) based on incident energy
- Qualified electrical workers only for energized work
- De-energized work preferred whenever possible

**Arc Flash PPE Levels:**
- **Level 0**: Untreated cotton clothing, safety glasses, hard hat
- **Level 1**: Arc-rated shirt/pants (4 cal/cm²), hard hat, safety glasses
- **Level 2**: Arc-rated shirt/pants (8 cal/cm²), arc-rated face shield
- **Level 3**: Arc-rated clothing (25 cal/cm²), arc-rated hood
- **Level 4**: Arc-rated clothing (40 cal/cm²), arc-rated suit

## 1.9 Ergonomics and Repetitive Stress Prevention

### Workstation Setup

#### Optimal Working Heights
**Standing Operations:**
- Work surface: Elbow height minus 2-4 inches
- Monitor: Top of screen at eye level or slightly below
- Frequently used controls: Between shoulder and waist height

**Seated Operations:**
- Chair height: Feet flat on floor or footrest
- Monitor distance: 20-26 inches from eyes
- Keyboard/mouse: Elbow angle 90-110 degrees
- Back support: Lumbar curve maintained

#### Tool Selection and Use
**Hand Tools:**
- Select tools that fit hand size
- Use tools with non-slip grips
- Maintain tools in good condition (sharp, properly adjusted)
- Alternate hands when possible for repetitive tasks

**Power Tools:**
- Use tools with vibration dampening when available
- Take breaks every 30 minutes for high-vibration tools
- Support tool weight with both hands or tool stands
- Maintain proper posture while using tools

### Lifting and Manual Handling

#### Safe Lifting Techniques
**Maximum Lifting Limits (Individual):**
- 50 lbs from floor to waist height
- 25 lbs from floor to shoulder height
- 10 lbs from floor to overhead

**Team Lift Required For:**
- Items over 50 lbs
- Awkward shapes that cannot be grasped properly
- Items that block vision during carry
- Distances over 50 feet

#### Proper Lifting Technique
1. **Plan the Lift**:
   - Clear path to destination
   - Secure firm footing
   - Get help if needed
   - Use mechanical aids when available

2. **Lifting Position**:
   - Squat down, don't bend at waist
   - Keep back straight and chest out
   - Grip with full hand, not fingertips
   - Hold load close to body

3. **The Lift**:
   - Lift with leg muscles
   - Rise smoothly without jerking
   - Keep load close to body
   - Don't twist while lifting

4. **Carrying**:
   - Keep load close to body
   - Maintain straight back
   - Change grip if needed during carry
   - Use two-person carry for long distances

### Repetitive Motion Injury Prevention

#### Common Repetitive Stress Injuries
**Carpal Tunnel Syndrome:**
- Symptoms: Numbness, tingling in thumb/fingers
- Prevention: Proper wrist position, regular breaks, stretching

**Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis):**
- Symptoms: Pain on outside of elbow
- Prevention: Proper tool grip, avoid repetitive wrist extension

**Trigger Finger:**
- Symptoms: Finger catching or locking
- Prevention: Avoid prolonged gripping, regular hand exercises

#### Break Schedule for Repetitive Tasks
**Micro-breaks**: 30 seconds every 10 minutes
- Relax hands and arms
- Simple stretching exercises
- Change position

**Short Breaks**: 5 minutes every hour
- Walk around work area
- Complete stretching routine
- Hydrate

**Long Breaks**: 15 minutes every 2 hours
- Leave workstation completely
- Perform full body stretches
- Rest and recover

#### Exercise Program for Machinists

**Hand and Wrist Exercises (Do every hour):**
1. **Wrist Circles**: 10 each direction
2. **Finger Extensions**: Spread fingers wide, hold 5 seconds
3. **Tendon Glides**: Make fist, then straight fingers, repeat 10 times
4. **Prayer Stretch**: Palms together, lower hands, hold 15 seconds

**Shoulder and Neck Exercises:**
1. **Shoulder Rolls**: 10 backward, 10 forward
2. **Neck Stretches**: Side to side, hold 15 seconds each
3. **Shoulder Blade Squeezes**: Pull shoulder blades together, hold 5 seconds

**Back and Leg Exercises:**
1. **Pelvic Tilts**: Tighten abdominal muscles, hold 5 seconds
2. **Hamstring Stretch**: Straight leg, reach for toes, hold 15 seconds
3. **Calf Raises**: Rise on toes, hold 2 seconds, repeat 15 times

## 1.10 Documentation and Incident Reporting

### Incident Classification

#### Near-Miss Events
**Definition**: An unplanned event that could have resulted in injury or damage
**Examples**:
- Tool dropping from spindle (no injury)
- Slip/trip with recovery (no fall)
- Machine malfunction with no damage
- Safety procedure not followed with no consequence

**Reporting Requirements**:
- Report within 2 hours of occurrence
- Complete near-miss report form
- Participate in investigation if requested
- No disciplinary action for good-faith reporting

#### First Aid Incidents
**Definition**: Minor injuries requiring only basic first aid treatment
**Examples**:
- Small cuts not requiring stitches
- Minor burns not requiring medical attention
- Eye irritation resolved with eyewash
- Bruises or minor sprains

**Reporting Requirements**:
- Report to supervisor immediately
- Log in first aid book
- Complete incident report within 24 hours
- Monitor for delayed symptoms

#### Medical Treatment Cases
**Definition**: Injuries requiring professional medical treatment
**Examples**:
- Cuts requiring stitches or medical glue
- Burns requiring medical treatment
- Eye injuries requiring medical examination
- Any injury resulting in restricted work

**Reporting Requirements**:
- Report immediately to supervisor and safety department
- Seek medical attention promptly
- Complete detailed incident report
- Participate fully in investigation
- Follow all return-to-work protocols

#### Lost Time Incidents
**Definition**: Injuries resulting in missed work time
**Reporting Requirements**:
- Immediate notification to management
- OSHA reporting within 8 hours if hospitalization required
- Complete investigation with root cause analysis
- Implement corrective actions before return to work

### Incident Report Documentation

#### Required Information for All Reports
1. **Personal Information**:
   - Full name and employee ID
   - Department and shift
   - Experience level and training completed
   - Contact information

2. **Incident Details**:
   - Date, time, and location
   - Equipment or materials involved
   - Weather conditions (if applicable)
   - Lighting conditions
   - Noise levels

3. **Description of Events**:
   - What happened (facts only, no opinions)
   - Sequence of events leading to incident
   - Actions taken after incident
   - Immediate causes identified

4. **Injury/Damage Information**:
   - Nature and extent of injuries
   - Body parts affected
   - Property damage description
   - Estimated repair/replacement costs

5. **Witness Information**:
   - Names and contact information
   - What each witness observed
   - Witness statements (signed and dated)

#### Root Cause Analysis

**Contributing Factors to Investigate**:
1. **Human Factors**:
   - Training adequacy
   - Experience level
   - Physical/mental condition
   - Understanding of procedures

2. **Equipment Factors**:
   - Machine condition and maintenance
   - Tool condition and suitability
   - Safety device functionality
   - Design adequacy

3. **Environmental Factors**:
   - Workspace organization
   - Lighting adequacy
   - Noise levels
   - Temperature conditions

4. **Procedural Factors**:
   - Procedure clarity and accuracy
   - Training on procedures
   - Enforcement of procedures
   - Procedure updates needed

### Safety Meeting Documentation

#### Daily Safety Meetings (Tailgate Talks)
**Required Topics** (rotate weekly):
- Week 1: PPE inspection and proper use
- Week 2: Machine-specific safety
- Week 3: Material handling and ergonomics
- Week 4: Chemical safety and MSDS review

**Documentation Requirements**:
- Date, time, and attendees
- Topic covered and presenter
- Questions asked and answers provided
- Action items identified
- Follow-up required

#### Monthly Safety Training
**Required Documentation**:
- Training objectives and content
- Attendee list with signatures
- Competency verification method
- Training effectiveness evaluation
- Certificates or records issued

### Safety Performance Metrics

#### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
1. **Days Without Lost Time Incident**: Target >90 days
2. **Near-Miss Reporting Rate**: Target >1 per employee per month
3. **Safety Training Compliance**: Target 100% current
4. **PPE Compliance Rate**: Target 100% during audits

#### Monthly Safety Reports
**Required Content**:
- Incident summary and trends
- Training completion status
- Safety audit results
- Corrective action status
- Recognition for good safety performance
