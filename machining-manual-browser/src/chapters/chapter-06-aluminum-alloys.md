## 6.1 Introduction to Aerospace Aluminum Alloys

### Why Aluminum Dominates Aerospace

Aluminum alloys represent approximately 70-80% of structural materials in modern aircraft. Their combination of strength, light weight, corrosion resistance, and machinability makes them ideal for aerospace applications.

**Key Advantages:**
- **High strength-to-weight ratio**: 2.5-3 times stronger than steel per unit weight
- **Excellent machinability**: High material removal rates with good surface finish
- **Corrosion resistance**: Natural oxide layer provides protection
- **Thermal conductivity**: Excellent heat dissipation during machining
- **Weldability**: Most alloys can be welded with proper techniques
- **Recycling**: 100% recyclable with minimal property loss

**Aerospace Applications:**
- **Fuselage frames and skins**: Primary structural components
- **Wing spars and ribs**: Load-bearing structural elements
- **Landing gear components**: High-strength applications
- **Engine components**: Non-hot section parts
- **Interior structures**: Seats, galleys, overhead bins

### Aluminum Alloy Classification System

#### Four-Digit Designation System
**First Digit**: Primary alloying element
- 1xxx: Pure aluminum (99%+ Al)
- 2xxx: Copper as primary alloying element
- 3xxx: Manganese as primary alloying element
- 4xxx: Silicon as primary alloying element
- 5xxx: Magnesium as primary alloying element
- 6xxx: Magnesium and Silicon as primary alloying elements
- 7xxx: Zinc as primary alloying element
- 8xxx: Other elements as primary alloying elements

**Second Digit**: Alloy modification
- 0: Original alloy
- 1-9: Alloy modifications

**Third and Fourth Digits**: Specific alloy identification within the series

#### Temper Designations
**F**: As fabricated (no special control over thermal or strain hardening)
**O**: Annealed (softest temper, fully recrystallized)
**H**: Strain hardened (work hardened, non-heat treatable alloys only)
**T**: Thermally treated (heat treatable alloys)

**Common T Tempers:**
- **T3**: Solution heat treated, cold worked, naturally aged
- **T4**: Solution heat treated, naturally aged
- **T6**: Solution heat treated, artificially aged
- **T651**: T6 + stress relieved by stretching

### Material Properties Critical for Machining

#### Thermal Properties
**Thermal Conductivity:**
- 2024: 120-150 W/m·K
- 6061: 150-180 W/m·K  
- 7075: 130-150 W/m·K

**Thermal Expansion:**
- All aluminum alloys: ~23 μm/m·K (12.9 μin/in·°F)
- 2.5 times greater expansion than steel
- Critical consideration for precision machining

#### Mechanical Properties (T6 Condition)
**2024-T6:**
- Tensile Strength: 65,000 PSI
- Yield Strength: 57,000 PSI
- Elongation: 6%
- Hardness: 120 HB

**6061-T6:**
- Tensile Strength: 45,000 PSI
- Yield Strength: 40,000 PSI
- Elongation: 12%
- Hardness: 95 HB

**7075-T6:**
- Tensile Strength: 83,000 PSI
- Yield Strength: 73,000 PSI
- Elongation: 11%
- Hardness: 150 HB

## 6.2 Alloy 2024 - High Strength Structural

### Composition and Properties

#### Chemical Composition (%)
- **Copper**: 3.8-4.9% (primary strengthening element)
- **Magnesium**: 1.2-1.8% (secondary strengthening)
- **Manganese**: 0.3-0.9% (grain refinement)
- **Silicon**: 0.50% max
- **Iron**: 0.50% max
- **Aluminum**: Remainder

#### Key Characteristics
**Strengths:**
- Excellent strength-to-weight ratio
- Good fatigue resistance
- Excellent machinability in T3 and T4 tempers
- Responds well to heat treatment
- Good formability in annealed condition

**Limitations:**
- Poor corrosion resistance (requires protection)
- Not weldable in high-strength tempers
- Susceptible to stress corrosion cracking
- Age hardens naturally at room temperature

### Heat Treatment Response

#### Solution Heat Treatment
**Process**: Heat to 935-950°F, quench in cold water
**Purpose**: Dissolve alloying elements into solid solution
**Timing**: Critical - quench delay causes precipitation

#### Aging Characteristics
**Natural Aging (T4):**
- Room temperature aging over weeks/months
- Reaches peak strength in ~4 days
- Continues to age slowly for years
- Final strength: ~65,000 PSI tensile

**Artificial Aging (T6):**
- Heat to 375°F for 8-12 hours
- Faster than natural aging
- Slightly lower ductility than T4
- More consistent properties

### Machining Characteristics

#### Machinability Rating
**2024-T3/T4**: Excellent (Rating: 9/10)
- Free-cutting with sharp tools
- High material removal rates possible
- Excellent surface finishes achievable
- Minimal tool wear

**2024-T6**: Good (Rating: 7/10)
- Slightly more challenging than T4
- Higher cutting forces required
- May work harden if cuts are too light
- Still very machinable compared to steel

#### Chip Formation
**T3/T4 Condition:**
- Long, continuous chips
- Good chip breaking with proper geometry
- Excellent for high-speed machining
- Requires good chip evacuation

**T6 Condition:**
- Shorter, more manageable chips
- Less tendency to gum up tools
- Good for conventional machining speeds
- Easier workholding due to higher strength

### Recommended Cutting Parameters

#### Turning Operations
**Rough Turning:**
- Speed: 800-1500 SFM
- Feed: 0.010-0.020 IPR
- Depth of Cut: 0.100-0.300"
- Tool: Carbide insert with sharp edge

**Finish Turning:**
- Speed: 1200-2000 SFM
- Feed: 0.005-0.012 IPR  
- Depth of Cut: 0.020-0.050"
- Tool: PCD or sharp carbide

#### Milling Operations
**Rough Milling:**
- Speed: 600-1200 SFM
- Feed: 0.005-0.015 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.200-0.500"
- Radial DOC: 50-75% of cutter diameter

**Finish Milling:**
- Speed: 1000-2000 SFM
- Feed: 0.003-0.008 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.050-0.100"
- Radial DOC: 5-15% of cutter diameter

#### Drilling Operations
**Standard Drilling:**
- Speed: 300-800 SFM
- Feed: 0.005-0.020 IPR (depending on diameter)
- Point Angle: 118° standard, 135° for harder tempers
- Use flood coolant for deep holes

**High-Speed Drilling:**
- Speed: 800-1500 SFM (small diameters)
- Feed: 0.003-0.010 IPR
- Carbide or PCD drills
- Excellent chip evacuation required

## 6.3 Alloy 6061 - General Purpose

### Composition and Properties

#### Chemical Composition (%)
- **Magnesium**: 0.8-1.2% (primary strengthening)
- **Silicon**: 0.4-0.8% (forms Mg₂Si precipitates)
- **Copper**: 0.15-0.40%
- **Chromium**: 0.04-0.35%
- **Iron**: 0.7% max
- **Aluminum**: Remainder

#### Key Characteristics
**Strengths:**
- Excellent corrosion resistance
- Good weldability in all conditions
- Excellent formability
- Good machinability
- Wide availability and low cost
- Responds well to anodizing

**Limitations:**
- Lower strength than 2024 or 7075
- Moderate fatigue resistance
- Cannot be strain hardened after solution heat treatment

### Heat Treatment Response

#### Solution Heat Treatment
**Process**: Heat to 970-990°F, quench in warm water (150-200°F)
**Purpose**: Dissolve Mg₂Si precipitates
**Characteristics**: Less critical timing than 2024

#### Aging Response
**Natural Aging (T4):**
- Slow aging at room temperature
- Reaches partial strength in weeks
- Used for forming operations
- Tensile strength: ~35,000 PSI

**Artificial Aging (T6):**
- Heat to 350°F for 8-12 hours
- Peak strength condition
- Tensile strength: ~45,000 PSI
- Most common delivery condition

### Machining Characteristics

#### Machinability Rating
**6061-T4**: Excellent (Rating: 9/10)
- Very free-cutting
- Excellent surface finish potential
- Minimal tool wear
- Good for complex machining operations

**6061-T6**: Very Good (Rating: 8/10)
- Still very machinable
- May require slightly reduced speeds from T4
- Good chip formation
- Excellent dimensional stability

#### Typical Applications
**General Aerospace Components:**
- Brackets and fittings
- Non-critical structural components
- Electrical enclosures
- Fuel system components
- Interior structural elements

### Recommended Cutting Parameters

#### Turning Operations
**Rough Turning (T6):**
- Speed: 800-1200 SFM
- Feed: 0.010-0.020 IPR
- Depth of Cut: 0.100-0.250"
- Tool: Carbide with positive rake

**Finish Turning (T6):**
- Speed: 1000-1800 SFM
- Feed: 0.005-0.012 IPR
- Depth of Cut: 0.020-0.050"
- Tool: PCD for best finish

#### Milling Operations
**Rough Milling (T6):**
- Speed: 500-1000 SFM
- Feed: 0.005-0.012 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.200-0.400"
- Radial DOC: 40-60% of cutter diameter

**Finish Milling (T6):**
- Speed: 800-1500 SFM
- Feed: 0.003-0.008 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.050-0.100"
- Radial DOC: 10-20% of cutter diameter

### Workholding Considerations

#### Clamping Guidelines
**T4 Condition:**
- Use light clamping pressure
- Multiple small clamps better than few large ones
- Watch for distortion during machining
- May need stress relief after machining

**T6 Condition:**
- Can use moderate clamping pressure
- Good dimensional stability
- Less prone to distortion
- Excellent for precision work

## 6.4 Alloy 7075 - Ultra High Strength

### Composition and Properties

#### Chemical Composition (%)
- **Zinc**: 5.1-6.1% (primary strengthening element)
- **Magnesium**: 2.1-2.9% (forms precipitates with zinc)
- **Copper**: 1.2-2.0% (additional strengthening)
- **Chromium**: 0.18-0.28% (grain structure control)
- **Iron**: 0.5% max
- **Silicon**: 0.4% max
- **Aluminum**: Remainder

#### Key Characteristics
**Strengths:**
- Highest strength of common aluminum alloys
- Excellent strength-to-weight ratio
- Good machinability when properly heat treated
- Excellent fatigue resistance
- Good at elevated temperatures (up to 200°F)

**Limitations:**
- Poor corrosion resistance
- Not weldable (cracks during welding)
- Expensive compared to other aluminum alloys
- Susceptible to stress corrosion cracking
- Quench sensitive during heat treatment

### Heat Treatment Response

#### Critical Heat Treatment Process
**Solution Heat Treatment:**
- Temperature: 900-920°F (very narrow range)
- Time: 1-2 hours depending on thickness
- Quench: Cold water (under 100°F)
- Timing: Must quench within 10 seconds of furnace removal

#### Aging Characteristics
**Natural Aging (T6):**
- Not used - results in poor properties
- Artificial aging required for strength

**Artificial Aging (T6):**
- **Step 1**: 250°F for 8 hours
- **Step 2**: 320°F for 8-24 hours
- **Result**: Peak strength ~83,000 PSI tensile
- **Critical**: Temperature control ±5°F

**Over-aging (T73):**
- Higher temperature aging (340-360°F)
- Lower strength (~73,000 PSI tensile)
- Better stress corrosion resistance
- Used for critical aerospace applications

### Machining Characteristics

#### Machinability Rating
**7075-T6**: Good (Rating: 7/10)
- Harder than 2024 or 6061
- Excellent dimensional stability
- Can work harden if cutting conditions are poor
- Requires sharp cutting tools

**7075-T73**: Very Good (Rating: 8/10)
- Slightly softer than T6
- Better chip formation
- Less prone to work hardening
- Preferred for complex machining

#### Chip Formation and Tool Wear
**Typical Chip Characteristics:**
- Short to medium length chips
- Good chip breaking characteristics
- Less gummy than softer aluminum alloys
- Higher cutting forces than 6061

**Tool Wear Patterns:**
- Moderate flank wear on carbide tools
- Excellent tool life with PCD
- Sharp cutting edges essential
- Built-up edge if speeds too low

### Recommended Cutting Parameters

#### Turning Operations
**Rough Turning (T6):**
- Speed: 600-1000 SFM
- Feed: 0.008-0.015 IPR
- Depth of Cut: 0.100-0.200"
- Tool: Carbide insert, sharp edge geometry

**Finish Turning (T6):**
- Speed: 800-1500 SFM
- Feed: 0.004-0.010 IPR
- Depth of Cut: 0.020-0.040"
- Tool: PCD for best results

#### Milling Operations
**Rough Milling (T6):**
- Speed: 400-800 SFM
- Feed: 0.004-0.010 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.150-0.300"
- Radial DOC: 30-50% of cutter diameter

**Finish Milling (T6):**
- Speed: 600-1200 SFM
- Feed: 0.002-0.006 IPT
- Axial DOC: 0.030-0.075"
- Radial DOC: 5-15% of cutter diameter

#### Drilling Operations
- Speed: 200-500 SFM
- Feed: 0.003-0.012 IPR
- Use carbide or HSS drills
- Peck drilling for deep holes
- Flood coolant recommended

### Critical Applications

#### Aerospace Uses
**Primary Structure:**
- Wing spars and bulkheads
- Fuselage frames
- Landing gear components
- High-stress brackets and fittings

**Requirements:**
- Full material traceability
- Heat treatment certification
- Non-destructive testing
- Stress corrosion testing for critical parts

## 6.5 Heat Treatment Effects on Machining

### Understanding Aluminum Metallurgy

#### Precipitation Hardening Mechanism
**Solution Treatment:**
- Alloying elements dissolved in aluminum matrix
- High temperature creates single-phase structure
- Rapid quench retains elements in supersaturated solution

**Aging Process:**
- Controlled precipitation of second-phase particles
- Precipitates obstruct dislocation movement
- Results in increased strength and hardness
- Affects machining characteristics

### Temper Effects on Machining

#### Annealed Condition (O)
**Characteristics:**
- Softest condition
- Excellent ductility
- Prone to gumming and built-up edge
- Difficult to achieve good surface finish

**Machining Approach:**
- Use very sharp tools
- Higher speeds to avoid built-up edge
- Light feeds to minimize work hardening
- Flood coolant to manage heat

#### Solution Treated (T4)
**Characteristics:**
- Intermediate hardness
- Good balance of strength and ductility
- Excellent machinability
- Natural aging occurs during machining

**Machining Considerations:**
- Properties change during natural aging
- Schedule rough machining first
- Finish machine after aging stabilizes
- Store in freezer to slow aging if needed

#### Peak Aged (T6)
**Characteristics:**
- Maximum strength condition
- Best dimensional stability
- Excellent machinability for aluminum
- Consistent properties

**Machining Benefits:**
- No property changes during machining
- Excellent dimensional stability
- Predictable cutting forces
- Best condition for precision work

#### Over-Aged (T73, T76)
**Characteristics:**
- Lower strength than T6
- Improved toughness and corrosion resistance
- Very stable properties
- Good machinability

**Applications:**
- Thick sections prone to residual stress
- Corrosive environments
- Critical safety applications

### Residual Stress Considerations

#### Sources of Residual Stress
**Heat Treatment Stresses:**
- Non-uniform cooling during quench
- Thermal gradients in thick sections
- Differential transformation stresses
- Constrained thermal contraction

**Machining-Induced Stresses:**
- Mechanical deformation from cutting forces
- Thermal stresses from cutting heat
- Clamping stresses during machining
- Tool wear effects

#### Stress Relief Methods
**Thermal Stress Relief:**
- Heat to 375-400°F for 2-8 hours
- Reduces residual stresses by 80-90%
- Minimal effect on mechanical properties
- Required for critical aerospace parts

**Mechanical Stress Relief:**
- Controlled plastic deformation
- Stretching (T651 temper designation)
- Shot peening for surface compression
- Machining stress relief cuts

### Heat Treatment Verification

#### Property Testing
**Hardness Testing:**
- Brinell or Rockwell B scale
- Quick verification of heat treatment
- Correlates with tensile strength
- Non-destructive screening method

**Tensile Testing:**
- Ultimate verification of properties
- Required for aerospace applications
- Destructive test - use witness coupons
- Full stress-strain curve data

#### Microstructure Analysis
**Grain Structure:**
- Average grain size affects machinability
- Fine grains improve surface finish
- Coarse grains may cause tear-out
- Metallographic examination required

**Precipitate Distribution:**
- Affects strength and machining behavior
- Over-aged material has coarse precipitates
- Under-aged has fine, coherent precipitates
- SEM analysis for detailed evaluation

## 6.6 Cutting Tool Selection

### Tool Material Selection

#### High-Speed Steel (HSS)
**Advantages:**
- Lowest cost option
- Tough and shock resistant
- Can be resharpened
- Good for interrupted cuts

**Limitations:**
- Low cutting speeds
- Poor for high-speed machining
- Shorter tool life than carbide

**Applications:**
- Manual machining operations
- Prototype and repair work
- Complex form tools
- Small hole drilling

#### Carbide Tools
**Uncoated Carbide:**
- Best general-purpose choice
- Sharp cutting edges possible
- Good wear resistance
- Moderate cost

**Coated Carbide:**
- TiN, TiCN, TiAlN coatings
- Improved tool life
- Can use higher cutting speeds
- More expensive than uncoated

**Carbide Grades for Aluminum:**
- **K01-K10**: Ultra-fine grain for finish cuts
- **K20-K30**: General purpose machining
- **K40**: Rough machining, interrupted cuts

#### Polycrystalline Diamond (PCD)
**Advantages:**
- Longest tool life (50-100x carbide)
- Excellent surface finish
- Very sharp cutting edges
- Chemically inert to aluminum

**Limitations:**
- High initial cost
- Cannot be resharpened
- Brittle - prone to chipping
- Not suitable for interrupted cuts

**Economic Justification:**
- High-volume production
- Critical surface finish requirements
- Long production runs
- Minimal setup time for tool changes

#### Tool Geometry Considerations

**Rake Angle:**
- **Positive rake**: 15-25° for aluminum
- Reduces cutting forces
- Improves surface finish
- Sharp cutting edge essential

**Clearance Angle:**
- **Primary clearance**: 8-12°
- **Secondary clearance**: 15-20°
- Prevents rubbing and heat buildup
- Critical for surface finish

**Cutting Edge Preparation:**
- **Sharp edge**: Best for aluminum
- **Slight hone**: 0.0002-0.0005" radius
- **Polished surfaces**: Reduces friction
- **Edge treatment**: May include coatings

### Tool Selection by Operation

#### Turning Tools
**Rough Turning:**
- Carbide insert: CCMT, CNMG geometries
- Positive rake angle: 15-20°
- Large nose radius for strength: 0.031-0.062"
- Chipbreaker for chip control

**Finish Turning:**
- PCD or sharp carbide insert
- Very sharp cutting edge
- Small nose radius: 0.008-0.016"
- Minimal cutting edge radius

**Threading:**
- Carbide threading inserts
- Sharp, polished cutting edges
- Proper thread form geometry
- Flood coolant recommended

#### Milling Tools
**End Mills for Aluminum:**
- 2-3 flutes for roughing
- 4-6 flutes for finishing
- Sharp, polished flutes
- Large gullets for chip evacuation

**Face Mills:**
- Carbide or PCD inserts
- Positive axial and radial rake
- Unequal spacing to reduce chatter
- Large chip gullets

**Specialty Tools:**
- Compression spiral bits for sheet
- Tapered ball nose for complex surfaces
- Chamfer mills for edge breaking

#### Drilling Tools
**Twist Drills:**
- HSS for general purpose
- Carbide for production
- 118° point angle standard
- Polished flutes for chip flow

**Specialized Drills:**
- Parabolic flute for deep holes
- Oil hole drills for through coolant
- Step drills for multiple diameters
- Gun drills for very deep holes

### Tool Wear and Failure Modes

#### Normal Wear Patterns
**Flank Wear:**
- Gradual wear on cutting edge
- Increases cutting forces
- Affects dimensional accuracy
- Monitor with microscope

**Crater Wear:**
- Wear on rake face
- More common with steel
- Minimal with aluminum
- PCD virtually immune

#### Premature Failure Modes
**Chipping:**
- Usually due to excessive feed
- Poor workholding causing vibration
- Tool material too brittle
- Interrupted cuts with PCD

**Built-Up Edge (BUE):**
- Aluminum welding to cutting edge
- Caused by insufficient speed
- Poor surface finish
- Use sharper tools, higher speeds

**Thermal Cracking:**
- Cyclical thermal stress
- Interrupted cuts at high speed
- Use tough tool materials
- Improve coolant flow

### Tool Life Optimization

#### Monitoring Methods
**Visual Inspection:**
- Check cutting edges between parts
- Look for chipping or wear
- Monitor surface finish changes
- Document tool condition

**Dimensional Monitoring:**
- Track part dimensions over time
- Increasing tool wear affects size
- Set replacement criteria
- Automated monitoring systems

#### Economic Tool Life
**Calculation Factors:**
- Tool cost per cutting edge
- Tool change time
- Machining time per part
- Scrap cost for worn tools

**Optimal Tool Life Formula:**
```
Optimal Life = √(Tool Change Time / Machining Time per Part)
```

#### Tool Life Extension
**Proper Storage:**
- Clean tools after use
- Protect cutting edges
- Control temperature and humidity
- Organize for easy identification

**Reconditioning:**
- Resharpen HSS tools when worn
- Remove chips and deposits
- Re-coat if applicable
- Quality control after reconditioning

## 6.7 Machining Parameters and Strategies

### Speed and Feed Calculations

#### Surface Speed (SFM) Calculations
**Formula**: SFM = (π × Diameter × RPM) / 12
**Turning Example**:
- Diameter: 2.000"
- Desired SFM: 1000
- RPM = (1000 × 12) / (π × 2.000) = 1909 RPM

**Milling Example**:
- End mill diameter: 0.500"
- Desired SFM: 800
- RPM = (800 × 12) / (π × 0.500) = 6111 RPM

#### Feed Rate Optimization
**Turning Feed Rates:**
- Roughing: 0.010-0.020 IPR
- Semi-finishing: 0.005-0.010 IPR
- Finishing: 0.002-0.008 IPR

**Milling Feed Rates:**
- Calculate: Feed Rate = RPM × Number of Teeth × Feed per Tooth
- Roughing: 0.005-0.015 IPT
- Finishing: 0.002-0.008 IPT

#### Depth of Cut Guidelines
**Turning Operations:**
- Roughing: 0.100-0.300" depth
- Finishing: 0.020-0.050" depth
- Consider tool and workpiece rigidity

**Milling Operations:**
- Axial depth: 0.200-0.500" roughing
- Radial depth: 0.100-0.200" roughing
- Finishing: Light axial and radial depths

### High-Speed Machining (HSM) Strategies

#### HSM Benefits for Aluminum
- Excellent heat dissipation properties
- Low cutting forces at high speeds
- Superior surface finishes achievable
- Reduced cycle times
- Less distortion due to lower cutting forces

#### HSM Requirements
**Machine Requirements:**
- High-speed spindles (10,000+ RPM)
- Excellent dynamic balance
- Rigid machine construction
- Advanced CNC control systems
- High-speed tool changers

**Tooling Requirements:**
- Carbide or PCD cutting tools
- Excellent balance and runout
- Short, rigid tool holders
- HSK or shrink-fit connections
- Dynamic balancing systems

#### HSM Programming Strategies
**Toolpath Planning:**
- Constant engagement milling
- Trochoidal milling for roughing
- Avoid slot milling when possible
- Smooth, flowing toolpaths

**Cutting Conditions:**
- Higher speeds, lower feeds per tooth
- Shallow axial depths of cut
- High radial engagement for roughing
- Climb milling preferred

### Conventional vs. Climb Milling

#### Conventional (Up) Milling
**Characteristics:**
- Chip thickness starts at zero
- Increases to maximum at exit
- Tendency to lift workpiece
- More spindle power required

**When to Use:**
- Older machines with backlash
- Workpieces that can't be clamped securely
- Surface with scale or hard layer
- Manual milling operations

#### Climb (Down) Milling
**Characteristics:**
- Chip thickness starts at maximum
- Decreases to zero at exit
- Pulls workpiece into cutter
- Better surface finish

**Advantages for Aluminum:**
- Superior surface finish
- Longer tool life
- Reduced work hardening
- Better chip evacuation

**Requirements:**
- Rigid machine and setup
- Minimal backlash in machine
- Secure workholding
- CNC machine preferred

### Machining Strategy Selection

#### Roughing Strategies
**Traditional Roughing:**
- High metal removal rates
- Large depth and width of cut
- Carbide tools with chipbreakers
- Flood coolant for heat dissipation

**High-Speed Roughing:**
- Light cuts at high speeds
- Constant tool engagement
- Excellent surface finish
- Reduced cutting forces

**Trochoidal Milling:**
- Constant radial engagement
- High axial depth of cut
- Smooth, circular toolpaths
- Excellent for difficult materials

#### Semi-Finishing Strategies
**Purpose**: Prepare surface for finish machining
**Typical Parameters:**
- 0.010-0.030" stock allowance
- Medium speeds and feeds
- Blend rough and finish conditions
- Verify dimensions before finishing

#### Finishing Strategies
**Objectives:**
- Meet final dimensional requirements
- Achieve specified surface finish
- Maintain tight tolerances
- Minimize distortion

**Approach:**
- Light cuts with sharp tools
- Consistent cutting conditions
- Excellent surface speed control
- Proper coolant application

### Adaptive Machining Techniques

#### Constant Surface Speed (CSS)
**Benefits:**
- Consistent tool performance
- Optimal cutting conditions maintained
- Improved tool life
- Better surface finish consistency

**Implementation:**
- CNC control automatically adjusts RPM
- As diameter changes, speed adjusts
- Particularly beneficial for turning
- Requires horsepower management

#### Constant Chip Load
**Principle**: Maintain consistent feed per tooth
**Benefits:**
- Consistent tool loading
- Predictable tool wear
- Optimal chip formation
- Improved productivity

#### Variable Helix End Mills
**Design**: Unequal helix angles between flutes
**Benefits:**
- Reduced chatter and vibration
- Improved surface finish
- Longer tool life
- Better performance in challenging setups

## 6.8 Workholding and Fixturing

### Clamping Force Considerations

#### Aluminum's Unique Characteristics
**Low Modulus of Elasticity:**
- Aluminum: 10 × 10⁶ PSI
- Steel: 30 × 10⁶ PSI
- More deflection under load
- Requires distributed clamping

**Soft Material Properties:**
- Easily damaged by over-clamping
- Prone to indentation and distortion
- Requires protective measures
- Careful clamping force control

#### Clamping Force Guidelines
**General Rules:**
- Use minimum force necessary
- Distribute loads over large areas
- Use soft jaws or protective pads
- Monitor for distortion during machining

**Force Calculations:**
```
Required Clamping Force = (Cutting Force × Safety Factor) / Friction Coefficient

Where:
- Safety Factor: 2-3 typically
- Friction Coefficient: 0.1-0.3 for aluminum on steel
```

### Fixture Design Principles

#### 3-2-1 Locating Principle
**Primary Datum (3 points):**
- Largest, most stable surface
- Three support points minimum
- Distribute points for stability
- Avoid high spots and irregularities

**Secondary Datum (2 points):**
- Perpendicular to primary datum
- Establishes orientation
- Use adjustable supports if needed
- Consider thermal expansion effects

**Tertiary Datum (1 point):**
- Completes part location
- Often edge or end surface
- May be moveable for part family

#### Fixture Materials
**Aluminum Fixtures:**
- Same thermal expansion as workpiece
- Lightweight and easy to machine
- Good for prototype and low-volume
- May wear quickly in production

**Steel Fixtures:**
- High strength and durability
- Better dimensional stability
- Heavier than aluminum
- Different thermal expansion

**Composite Materials:**
- Carbon fiber for ultra-light fixtures
- Good dimensional stability
- Expensive but beneficial for large parts
- Non-conductive properties

### Workholding Methods

#### Machine Vises
**Standard Vises:**
- Good for prismatic parts
- Easy setup and operation
- Limited to simple geometries
- May require custom soft jaws

**Sine Vises:**
- Precise angular positioning
- Good for angled surfaces
- Requires careful setup
- Limited clamping force

**Multi-Station Vises:**
- Multiple parts in single setup
- Reduced setup time
- Consistent part location
- Good for production runs

#### Custom Fixtures
**Advantages:**
- Optimized for specific part
- Maximum productivity
- Repeatable setup
- Can machine multiple operations

**Design Considerations:**
- Access for all required operations
- Tool clearance verification
- Chip evacuation provisions
- Loading/unloading ergonomics

#### Vacuum Workholding
**Ideal Applications:**
- Thin sheet and plate
- Large flat surfaces
- Multiple small parts
- Delicate or easily damaged parts

**System Requirements:**
- Adequate vacuum pump capacity
- Smooth, sealed work surface
- Backup clamping for safety
- Vacuum monitoring system

**Advantages:**
- No distortion from clamping
- Quick part loading/unloading
- Access to entire part surface
- Suitable for automation

### Distortion Prevention

#### Causes of Part Distortion
**Clamping-Induced Stress:**
- Excessive clamping force
- Concentrated contact points
- Uneven support distribution
- Different thermal expansion

**Machining-Induced Stress:**
- Cutting forces and vibration
- Heat generation during cutting
- Non-uniform material removal
- Tool wear effects

**Residual Stress Relief:**
- Heat treatment stresses
- Released during machining
- Causes dimensional changes
- Requires stress relief operations

#### Distortion Minimization Strategies
**Fixturing Approach:**
- Use multiple, light clamps
- Soft jaws with large contact area
- Support thin sections adequately
- Balance clamping forces

**Machining Strategy:**
- Rough machine first, allow stress relief
- Take light finishing cuts
- Balance material removal
- Use consistent cutting conditions

**Part Design:**
- Adequate part rigidity
- Symmetric material removal
- Stress relief features
- Proper heat treatment

### Special Workholding Situations

#### Thin-Walled Parts
**Challenges:**
- Extremely low rigidity
- Prone to vibration and chatter
- Easy to over-clamp and distort
- Difficult to achieve tolerances

**Solutions:**
- Internal support (expandable mandrels)
- Wax or low-melting-point alloy backing
- Vacuum workholding
- Multiple light clamps

#### Large Plates and Sheets
**Support Requirements:**
- Continuous support to prevent sagging
- Thermal expansion accommodation
- Easy loading and unloading
- Good chip evacuation

**Fixturing Methods:**
- Pin and clamp systems
- Vacuum tables
- Modular fixturing systems
- Custom support frameworks

#### Complex Geometries
**Multi-Operation Fixtures:**
- Position part for multiple setups
- Maintain coordinate relationship
- Quick changeover between operations
- Modular design for flexibility

**Tombstone Fixtures:**
- Multiple parts per setup
- Different operations simultaneously
- Maximizes machine utilization
- Requires careful coordination

## 6.9 Coolant and Lubrication

### Coolant Functions in Aluminum Machining

#### Primary Functions
**Heat Removal:**
- Prevents thermal damage to workpiece
- Extends tool life significantly
- Maintains dimensional accuracy
- Reduces thermal stress and distortion

**Chip Evacuation:**
- Flushes chips from cutting zone
- Prevents chip recutting
- Reduces surface finish problems
- Essential for deep hole operations

**Lubrication:**
- Reduces friction between tool and chip
- Improves surface finish
- Reduces cutting forces
- Prevents built-up edge formation

### Coolant Types for Aluminum

#### Water-Based Coolants
**Synthetic Coolants:**
- No oil content (true solutions)
- Excellent cooling properties
- Good corrosion protection
- Low foaming tendency

**Semi-Synthetic Coolants:**
- Oil-in-water emulsions with additives
- Good balance of cooling and lubrication
- Moderate cost
- Popular for aluminum machining

**Soluble Oils:**
- Traditional oil-in-water emulsions
- Excellent lubrication properties
- Good for heavy cuts
- May stain aluminum parts

#### Straight Cutting Oils
**Mineral-Based Oils:**
- Excellent lubrication
- Good for finishing operations
- No corrosion issues
- Fire hazard at high temperatures

**Synthetic Oils:**
- Superior performance
- Better thermal stability
- Higher cost
- Environmentally friendly options

#### Minimum Quantity Lubrication (MQL)
**System Description:**
- Micro-amounts of lubricant in air stream
- Applied directly to cutting zone
- Typically 0.03-0.5 ml/minute
- Environmentally friendly approach

**Advantages:**
- Reduced coolant costs
- Cleaner machining environment
- No coolant disposal issues
- Excellent for high-speed machining

**Limitations:**
- Limited heat removal capability
- Not suitable for heavy cuts
- Requires compatible lubricants
- May need supplemental air blast

### Coolant Application Methods

#### Flood Cooling
**Description**: Continuous coolant flow over cutting area
**Advantages:**
- Maximum heat removal
- Excellent chip flushing
- Simple system design
- Suitable for all operations

**System Requirements:**
- Adequate pump capacity: 3-5 GPM minimum
- Proper filtration: 25-50 micron
- Temperature control: 70-80°F optimal
- Regular maintenance and monitoring

#### High-Pressure Coolant
**Applications:**
- Deep hole drilling
- Heavy roughing operations
- Difficult-to-machine alloys
- Chip breaking and evacuation

**System Specifications:**
- Pressure: 300-1500 PSI
- Flow rate: 0.5-5 GPM
- Special pumping systems required
- Through-tool coolant delivery

#### Mist Cooling
**System Description:**
- Coolant atomized in air stream
- Lower coolant consumption
- Good for light cuts
- Cleaner work environment

**Applications:**
- High-speed finishing
- Small diameter tools
- Shallow cuts
- Parts requiring clean surfaces

### Coolant Management

#### Concentration Control
**Monitoring Methods:**
- Refractometer: Most accurate method
- pH testing: Indicates bacterial growth
- Visual inspection: Color and clarity
- Titration: Chemical analysis method

**Typical Concentrations:**
- Synthetic coolants: 3-8%
- Semi-synthetic: 5-10%
- Soluble oils: 5-15%
- Follow manufacturer recommendations

#### Bacterial Control
**Prevention Methods:**
- Maintain proper concentration
- Monitor pH levels (8.8-9.2 target)
- Good system cleanliness
- Adequate circulation and aeration

**Bacterial Testing:**
- Weekly dip slide testing
- Bacterial count <10⁶ CFU/ml acceptable
- >10⁶ CFU/ml requires system cleaning
- Document all test results

#### Filtration Systems
**Mechanical Filtration:**
- Removes chips and particles
- 25-50 micron typical
- Paper, cloth, or wire mesh media
- Automatic cleaning systems available

**Magnetic Separation:**
- Removes ferrous particles
- Important for mixed material shops
- Magnetic drums or separators
- Extends coolant life significantly

**Centrifugal Separation:**
- High-efficiency particle removal
- Good for fine particles
- Continuous operation
- Higher initial cost

### Troubleshooting Coolant Issues

#### Common Problems
**Poor Surface Finish:**
- Check coolant concentration
- Verify adequate flow rate
- Test for bacterial contamination
- Consider coolant type change

**Tool Wear Issues:**
- Insufficient lubrication
- Wrong coolant type for application
- Contaminated coolant system
- Inadequate flow or pressure

**Corrosion Problems:**
- pH too low (bacterial growth)
- Inadequate corrosion inhibitors
- Galvanic corrosion from mixed metals
- Poor coolant maintenance

#### Coolant System Maintenance
**Daily Tasks:**
- Check coolant level and concentration
- Visual inspection for contamination
- Monitor system operation
- Clean chips from system

**Weekly Tasks:**
- Bacterial testing
- pH measurement and adjustment
- System cleaning and maintenance
- Filter replacement as needed

**Monthly Tasks:**
- Complete coolant analysis
- System drainage and cleaning if needed
- Equipment calibration
- Preventive maintenance activities

### Environmental and Safety Considerations

#### Health and Safety
**Exposure Limits:**
- OSHA PEL for oil mist: 5 mg/m³
- Respiratory protection if limits exceeded
- Skin protection and hygiene
- Eye protection required

**Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS):**
- Review before use
- Understand health hazards
- Know emergency procedures
- Train operators properly

#### Environmental Compliance
**Waste Disposal:**
- Used coolant is hazardous waste
- Licensed disposal required
- Documentation and manifests
- Recycling options available

**Water Discharge:**
- May require treatment before discharge
- pH adjustment typically required
- Oil/water separation
- Local regulations apply

## 6.10 Surface Finish and Quality Control

### Surface Finish Requirements in Aerospace

#### Common Aerospace Finishes
**Structural Components:**
- Ra 125 μin (3.2 μm): General structural surfaces
- Ra 63 μin (1.6 μm): Improved structural surfaces
- Ra 32 μin (0.8 μm): Critical structural interfaces

**Sealing Surfaces:**
- Ra 16 μin (0.4 μm): O-ring grooves and gasket surfaces
- Ra 8 μin (0.2 μm): High-pressure sealing applications
- Ra 4 μin (0.1 μm): Ultra-precision sealing surfaces

**Bearing Surfaces:**
- Ra 16-32 μin: Journal bearings and bushings
- Ra 8-16 μin: Rolling element bearing races
- Ra 4-8 μin: Precision bearing applications

### Factors Affecting Surface Finish

#### Tool-Related Factors
**Cutting Edge Condition:**
- Sharp edges produce better finishes
- Tool wear increases surface roughness
- Built-up edge causes poor finish
- Chipped edges create surface defects

**Tool Geometry:**
- Large nose radius improves finish
- Positive rake angle reduces cutting forces
- Proper clearance angles prevent rubbing
- Tool material affects surface quality

**Feed Rate Effects:**
- Lower feeds generally improve finish
- Theoretical finish = Feed² / (8 × Nose Radius)
- Practical finish affected by other factors
- Optimize for both finish and productivity

#### Material-Related Factors
**Aluminum Alloy Characteristics:**
- Softer alloys may "smear" during cutting
- Heat-treatable alloys machine better in aged condition
- Grain structure affects surface texture
- Precipitate distribution influences finish

**Microstructure Effects:**
- Fine grain structure improves finish
- Coarse grains may cause "orange peel" effect
- Second-phase particles can pull out
- Heat treatment optimization important

#### Process-Related Factors
**Cutting Conditions:**
- Higher speeds generally improve finish
- Consistent cutting conditions critical
- Avoid interrupted cuts when possible
- Maintain sharp cutting tools

**Machine Condition:**
- Spindle runout affects finish
- Vibration causes chatter marks
- Thermal effects influence stability
- Rigidity essential for smooth cutting

### Surface Finish Measurement

#### Measurement Parameters
**Ra (Arithmetic Average):**
- Most common parameter
- Average absolute deviation from mean line
- Good for general surface characterization
- Easy to measure and understand

**Rz (Average Maximum Height):**
- Average peak-to-valley height
- Better for functional surfaces
- More sensitive to surface defects
- Preferred for some aerospace applications

**Rq (Root Mean Square):**
- Statistical measure of roughness
- More sensitive to large deviations
- Better correlation with some functions
- Used for precision applications

#### Measurement Techniques
**Contact Profilometry:**
- Stylus traces surface profile
- High accuracy and repeatability
- Standard method for aerospace
- Requires proper technique

**Optical Profilometry:**
- Non-contact measurement
- Fast measurement capability
- Good for delicate surfaces
- Limited by surface reflectivity

**Comparison Standards:**
- Visual/tactile comparison
- Quick shop floor method
- Limited accuracy
- Good for sorting and screening

### Quality Control Procedures

#### Incoming Material Inspection
**Material Certification:**
- Verify material grade and temper
- Check heat treatment certification
- Review test data and properties
- Confirm traceability documentation

**Physical Inspection:**
- Check for surface defects
- Measure dimensions and tolerances
- Verify straightness and flatness
- Document any non-conformances

#### In-Process Quality Control
**First Article Inspection:**
- Complete dimensional verification
- Surface finish measurement
- Material property verification
- Process capability assessment

**Statistical Process Control:**
- Monitor key characteristics
- Control charts for dimensions
- Track surface finish trends
- Process capability studies

#### Final Inspection
**Dimensional Verification:**
- CMM measurement for complex parts
- Hand tool verification for simple features
- GD&T evaluation and reporting
- Comparison to drawing requirements

**Surface Quality Assessment:**
- Surface finish measurement
- Visual inspection for defects
- Corrosion resistance verification
- Cleanliness requirements

### Common Surface Defects

#### Machining-Related Defects
**Chatter Marks:**
- Regular spacing patterns
- Caused by machine vibration
- Affects both appearance and function
- Requires machine/setup modification

**Feed Marks:**
- Regular spacing equal to feed rate
- Normal for turned surfaces
- May require polishing for critical applications
- Control with proper feeds and speeds

**Tool Marks:**
- Irregular scratches or gouges
- Caused by damaged cutting tools
- Replace or recondition tools
- May require rework of affected surfaces

#### Material-Related Defects
**Grain Pull-Out:**
- Small voids in surface
- More common in coarse-grained materials
- Use sharper tools and lighter cuts
- May require different heat treatment

**Orange Peel Effect:**
- Wavy surface appearance
- Related to grain structure
- Difficult to eliminate by machining
- May require surface treatment

**Inclusions and Voids:**
- Foreign material in aluminum
- Porosity from casting or forging
- Cannot be eliminated by machining
- May require material replacement

### Surface Treatment Options

#### Mechanical Treatments
**Polishing:**
- Progressive abrasive grits
- Removes machining marks
- Achieves mirror finishes
- Labor intensive process

**Shot Peening:**
- Compressive surface stress
- Improves fatigue resistance
- Uniform surface texture
- Controlled process parameters

**Vibratory Finishing:**
- Batch processing method
- Consistent surface finish
- Good for complex geometries
- Various media available

#### Chemical Treatments
**Chemical Etching:**
- Removes thin surface layer
- Uniform material removal
- Good for complex shapes
- Requires careful process control

**Anodizing Preparation:**
- Alkaline cleaning
- Acid etching
- Removes surface contaminants
- Prepares for anodizing process

#### Coating Applications
**Anodizing:**
- Electrochemical oxidation
- Improves corrosion resistance
- Decorative finishes available
- Thickness control critical

**Paint and Primers:**
- Corrosion protection
- Aesthetic requirements
- Requires surface preparation
- Multiple coat systems

## 6.11 Troubleshooting Common Problems

### Dimensional Problems

#### Size Variations
**Symptoms:**
- Parts consistently over/under size
- Size varies throughout production run
- Different operators get different results

**Potential Causes:**
1. **Tool wear**: Cutting tools gradually wear during use
2. **Thermal effects**: Temperature changes affect part size
3. **Machine deflection**: Cutting forces bend machine structure
4. **Setup errors**: Incorrect tool offsets or work coordinates

**Investigation Steps:**
1. **Check tool condition**: Measure cutting edge wear
2. **Verify cutting conditions**: Compare to recommended parameters
3. **Monitor temperatures**: Part, tool, and machine temperatures
4. **Measure machine deflection**: Use dial indicators during cutting

**Solutions:**
- **Tool wear compensation**: Adjust offsets as tools wear
- **Temperature control**: Maintain consistent shop temperature
- **Improve rigidity**: Reduce overhang, increase support
- **Setup verification**: Double-check all offset values

#### Surface Location Accuracy
**Symptoms:**
- Surfaces not perpendicular or parallel as required
- Holes not positioned correctly
- Assembly problems with mating parts

**Potential Causes:**
1. **Machine geometry errors**: Worn ways, spindle issues
2. **Setup problems**: Improper work coordinate establishment
3. **Workholding issues**: Part moves during machining
4. **Tool deflection**: Cutting forces bend long tools

**Investigation Process:**
1. **Machine verification**: Check with precision instruments
2. **Setup audit**: Verify all setup procedures
3. **Workholding check**: Look for clamping movement
4. **Tool analysis**: Calculate deflection, check rigidity

### Surface Finish Problems

#### Poor Surface Finish
**Symptoms:**
- Rough, torn, or built-up surface
- Chatter marks or irregular patterns
- Surface roughness exceeds specifications

**Common Causes and Solutions:**

**Built-Up Edge (BUE):**
- **Cause**: Cutting speed too low, dull tools
- **Solution**: Increase speed, use sharper tools, improve coolant

**Chatter:**
- **Cause**: Insufficient rigidity, resonance
- **Solution**: Change speeds/feeds, improve support, damping

**Tool Wear:**
- **Cause**: Excessive cutting conditions, wrong tool material
- **Solution**: Reduce parameters, use appropriate tools

**Feed Marks:**
- **Cause**: Feed rate too high for required finish
- **Solution**: Reduce feed rate, increase nose radius

#### Inconsistent Surface Finish
**Symptoms:**
- Finish varies across surface
- Different finishes on similar features
- Random variations in surface quality

**Investigation Approach:**
1. **Tool condition audit**: Check all cutting tools
2. **Machine condition**: Verify spindle, ways, etc.
3. **Material variation**: Check for hardness differences
4. **Process consistency**: Verify cutting parameters

### Tool Life Problems

#### Premature Tool Failure
**Symptoms:**
- Tools fail before expected life
- Excessive tool costs
- Frequent tool changes disrupting production

**Failure Analysis Process:**
1. **Examine failed tools**: Identify failure mode
2. **Review cutting conditions**: Compare to recommendations
3. **Check setup rigidity**: Look for vibration sources
4. **Material verification**: Confirm alloy and temper

**Common Failure Modes:**

**Chipping:**
- **Causes**: Excessive feed, interrupted cuts, vibration
- **Solutions**: Reduce feed, use tougher tools, improve setup

**Crater Wear:**
- **Causes**: High cutting temperatures, chemical interaction
- **Solutions**: Reduce speed, improve coolant, use coated tools

**Flank Wear:**
- **Causes**: Abrasive particles, wrong cutting speed
- **Solutions**: Improve filtration, optimize speed

#### Excessive Tool Wear Variation
**Symptoms:**
- Large variation in tool life between identical tools
- Inconsistent performance from same tool type
- Unpredictable tool replacement schedules

**Improvement Strategies:**
1. **Standardize procedures**: Consistent setup and operation
2. **Tool quality control**: Inspect new tools before use
3. **Operator training**: Ensure consistent techniques
4. **Documentation**: Track and analyze tool performance

### Workholding Problems

#### Part Movement During Machining
**Symptoms:**
- Dimensional variations during cutting
- Visible part movement
- Inconsistent surface finishes
- Safety hazards

**Investigation Steps:**
1. **Clamping force verification**: Check all clamps
2. **Contact point inspection**: Verify full engagement
3. **Cutting force analysis**: Calculate forces vs. holding
4. **Vibration assessment**: Look for resonance issues

**Solutions:**
- **Increase clamping force**: Within safe limits for aluminum
- **Improve contact**: Use soft jaws, larger contact areas
- **Add support**: Additional clamps or rests
- **Reduce cutting forces**: Lower feeds and speeds

#### Part Distortion
**Symptoms:**
- Parts warp after removal from fixture
- Dimensions change during machining sequence
- Assembly problems due to shape changes

**Distortion Analysis:**
1. **Stress source identification**: Clamping, cutting, residual
2. **Measurement timing**: When does distortion occur?
3. **Material factors**: Heat treatment, grain structure
4. **Process sequence**: Order of operations effect

**Prevention Strategies:**
- **Stress relief**: Heat treatment before final machining
- **Balanced machining**: Remove material symmetrically
- **Light clamping**: Minimum force necessary
- **Support structures**: Internal supports for thin walls

### Chip Management Issues

#### Poor Chip Evacuation
**Symptoms:**
- Chips accumulate in cutting area
- Surface finish degradation
- Tool damage from chip recutting
- Operator safety concerns

**Improvement Methods:**
1. **Coolant optimization**: Increase flow and pressure
2. **Tool selection**: Better chip breaking geometry
3. **Programming changes**: Modify toolpaths for chip flow
4. **Auxiliary systems**: Air blast, chip conveyors

#### Chip Welding to Tools
**Symptoms:**
- Built-up edge on cutting tools
- Poor surface finish
- Accelerated tool wear
- Dimensional problems

**Solutions:**
- **Increase cutting speed**: Reduce welding tendency
- **Improve lubrication**: Better coolant or MQL
- **Tool coatings**: Reduce aluminum adhesion
- **Tool geometry**: More positive rake angles

### Quality Control Issues

#### Measurement Repeatability
**Symptoms:**
- Different measurements on same feature
- Operator-to-operator variations
- CMM results don't match manual measurements

**Improvement Process:**
1. **Calibration verification**: Check all measurement tools
2. **Technique standardization**: Train all operators consistently
3. **Environmental control**: Minimize temperature variations
4. **Measurement uncertainty**: Analyze and minimize sources

#### Out-of-Tolerance Conditions
**Symptoms:**
- Parts failing final inspection
- Customer complaints about quality
- High scrap and rework rates

**Root Cause Analysis:**
1. **Process capability study**: Determine if process is capable
2. **Measurement system analysis**: Verify measurement quality
3. **Statistical analysis**: Identify patterns and trends
4. **Corrective action**: Address root causes systematically

### Preventive Measures

#### Maintenance Programs
**Preventive Maintenance:**
- Regular machine calibration
- Cutting tool inspection and replacement
- Coolant system maintenance
- Workholding system verification

**Predictive Maintenance:**
- Vibration monitoring
- Tool wear trending
- Statistical process control
- Performance tracking

#### Training and Documentation
**Operator Training:**
- Proper setup procedures
- Cutting parameter selection
- Quality control techniques
- Problem identification skills

**Process Documentation:**
- Standard operating procedures
- Setup sheets and tooling lists
- Inspection requirements
